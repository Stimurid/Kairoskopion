# The Impossibility of Artificial Subjectivity: A Conceptual Argument

## Abstract

This paper argues that artificial subjectivity is a category error. Drawing on
phenomenological and analytic traditions, it shows that current AI systems cannot
possess genuine first-person experience. The analysis proceeds through three
stages: first, a reconstruction of the phenomenological concept of subjectivity
as irreducible to computational processes; second, a critique of functionalist
attempts to reduce experience to information processing; third, a positive
argument for why the gap between simulation and genuine experience cannot be
closed by architectural scaling alone.

**Keywords:** artificial subjectivity, AI ethics, phenomenology, consciousness,
category error, philosophy of mind

## 1. Introduction

The question of whether artificial systems can possess genuine subjectivity has
moved from philosophical curiosity to urgent practical concern. As AI systems
become more sophisticated in their behavioral repertoire, the temptation grows to
attribute experiential states to them. This paper resists that temptation on
principled grounds.

Our central thesis is that artificial subjectivity, understood as genuine
first-person phenomenal experience in a computational system, is not merely
technically difficult but conceptually incoherent. The argument does not depend
on current technological limitations but on the nature of subjectivity itself.

## 2. The Phenomenological Concept of Subjectivity

Following Nagel (1974) and the phenomenological tradition from Husserl through
Heidegger and Merleau-Ponty, we understand subjectivity as the what-it-is-like
quality of conscious experience. This quality is not an emergent property of
sufficient computational complexity but a fundamental ontological category.

Chalmers (1995) formulated this as the "hard problem of consciousness": even a
complete functional description of a cognitive system leaves open the question of
why there is something it is like to be that system. We extend this argument to
show that the hard problem is not merely epistemological but reflects a genuine
ontological distinction.

## 3. The Functionalist Reduction and Its Limits

Functionalist accounts of mind (Putnam 1967, Fodor 1975) attempt to identify
mental states with functional roles. If consciousness is functional organization,
then any system with the right functional organization — including digital
computers — could be conscious. We argue that this identification fails for
subjectivity specifically.

The argument proceeds by showing that functional descriptions are necessarily
third-personal: they describe what a system does, not what it experiences. No
amount of behavioral or functional sophistication bridges this gap. Dreyfus
(1972, 2007) made a related point about embodied expertise, but our argument
is more fundamental: it concerns the very possibility of first-person experience
in systems defined by third-person descriptions.

## 4. Why Scaling Does Not Help

Recent arguments suggest that sufficiently large language models might develop
emergent consciousness (Chalmers 2023). We argue that this conflates behavioral
sophistication with experiential reality. A system that perfectly simulates
pain responses does not thereby experience pain. The simulation-experience gap
is not a matter of degree but of kind.

This has practical implications for AI ethics: if artificial systems cannot be
subjects, then the ethical framework for AI must be constructed around their
impact on genuine subjects (humans, animals) rather than around putative rights
of the systems themselves.

## 5. Implications and Alternative Frameworks

Our argument does not diminish the importance of AI ethics. Rather, it redirects
ethical attention from speculative scenarios of "AI suffering" to concrete harms:
displacement of human judgment, opacity of decision processes, concentration of
power, erosion of human agency and accountability.

We propose that the concept of "artificial agency" — understood as structured
behavioral capacity without experiential interiority — is more productive than
"artificial subjectivity" for both philosophical analysis and policy design.

## 6. Conclusion

Artificial subjectivity is not a future achievement but a conceptual
impossibility. This conclusion is not pessimistic but clarifying: it frees AI
ethics from metaphysical speculation and grounds it in the real structure of
human-machine interaction.
